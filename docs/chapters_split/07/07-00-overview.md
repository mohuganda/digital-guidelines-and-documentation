Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)

ICD10 CODE: N18

Chronic impairment of kidney function

Causes/risk factors

Diabetes mellitus

Hypertension/cardiovascular disease

Age >50 years

Kidney stones

Drugs especially pain killers like diclofenac, ibuprofen and
other NSAIDs

Family history of kidney disease

HIV/AIDS

Clinical features

Most patients with CKD have no symptoms until the disease is advanced

May present with features of predisposing risk factor

Anaemia, lethargy, easy fatigue, appetite loss, nausea,
vomiting, skin itching, bone pains

May have body swelling
May have loin pain

Differential diagnosis

Other causes of chronic anaemia
Heart failure
Protein-energy malnutrition
Chronic liver disease

Investigations

 Creatinine/Urea/electrolytes
 Urine dip stick for protein and blood
 Kidney ultrasound
 Urine dipsticks (for protein and blood) and blood pressure

measurement at least once a year in high risk patients

 In diabetics, urine microalbumin where possible or a spot

urine for protein: creatinine ratio at least once a year

 Patients with detected abnormalities should have a serum

creatinine test performed and GFR calculated as suggested
above

Refer the following patients for specialist attention:

Children
Persistent proteinuria or haematuria beyond 3 months

GFR <60 ml/min or creatinine >1.9 mg/dl

Familial kidney disease, e.g. polycystic kidney disease

Establish diagnosis and treat reversible diseases

7.1.3

Use of Drugs in Renal Failure
Be very careful when prescribing any medicine and check
available prescribing information (e.g. in Practical Guidelines for Dispensing 2015) regarding use in renal failure/
impairment
Many medicines are excreted through the kidneys and acc
mulate when urinary output is reduced
Some drugs are presented as sodium or potassium salts
and contribute to accumulation of these electrolytes
With life-threatening infections (e.g. meningitis), use normal
or high doses of antibiotics initially, and then reduce doses
once the condition has responded
