- Peptic/duodenal ulcers Clinical features

- Acute abdominal pain usually in the epigastrium radiating

to the back

- Pain worsened by eating or lying down and relieved by

sitting up or leaning forward

- Nausea, vomiting, abdominal distension

- Fever, tachycardia, dehydration (may be severely ill with

shock)

- Abdomen is very tender but in the absence of peritonitis

there is no rigidity/rebound tenderness Complications

- Pseudocysts

- Necrotizing pancreatitis with infection

- Peritonitis Differential diagnosis

- Perforated peptic ulcer, peritonitis

- Acute cholecystitis, inflammation of the biliary tract

- Sickle-cell anaemia crisis Investigations

 Blood: Serum analysis, complete blood count, random blood sugar  Raised pancreatic amylase and lipase > 3 times normal  Ultrasound: gallstones, pancreatic oedema, abdominal fluid  Liver function tests: raised liver enzymes
