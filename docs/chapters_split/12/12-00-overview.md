# Chapter 12: Oncology

Cancer is an unregulated growth of a previously normal set of body
cells. Oncology is the study, diagnosis, and management of cancers or
tumours. It is important to note that any organ or system as well as
any individual can be
affected by cancer. This section will outline major symptoms and signs
of cancer, key population groups affected, ways to mitigate risk of cancer
and provide an overview of common cancers in adults and children.
Cancer or malignant neoplasm is collective term for a group of more
than 100 diseases that result from abnormal / uncontrolled growth
of body cells and is able to invade normal tissues and spread to other
parts of the body. The uncontrolled growth causes a lump/ swelling
called a tumours or neoplasm in many types of cancer or an abnormal
number of abnormal cells in some types of cancer such as blood cancers
(Leukaemia). Tumours are broadly divided into benign tumors, meaning
non-cancerous / unable to metastasize (spread to other parts of the body)
or malignant tumors (able to invade normal tissues / spread to other
parts of the body). Cancers are classified by their type of cell, tissue, or
organ of origin. In Uganda, in 2020, it was estimated that there were
34,008 new cancer cases, 22,992 cancer deaths, and 62,548 adults
living with cancer. The top five causes of cancer morbidity are cervix,
Kaposi sarcoma (KS), breast, prostate, and non-Hodgkin lymphoma.
The top five causes of cancer deaths are cancers of the cervix, KS,
esophagus, liver, and non-Hodgkin lymphoma. In Uganda, children aged
0 – 14 years of age, constitute ten percent (10%) of cancer patients.
